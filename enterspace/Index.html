The Sirius

Sirius, also known as the Dog Star, is the brightest star in the night sky and is part of the Canis Major constellation. Located approximately 8.6 light-years away, it is one of the closest stars to Earth. Sirius is actually a binary star system, consisting of Sirius A, a bright main-sequence star, and Sirius B, a faint white dwarf that was once a massive star. With a surface temperature of around 9,940 K, Sirius A shines brilliantly, making it nearly twice as bright as the second-brightest star, Canopus. Throughout history, Sirius has held great cultural significance.

EBLM J0555

EBLM J0555-57Ab is one of the smallest known stars that can sustain hydrogen fusion, making it a fascinating object in stellar astrophysics. It is located in a binary star system about 600 light-years away in the constellation Pictor. This star is roughly the size of Saturn, making it one of the tiniest main-sequence stars ever discovered. Despite its small size, it has enough mass to sustain nuclear fusion, the process that powers stars like the Sun.
